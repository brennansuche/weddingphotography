The Wedding Day Timeline Guide
From a Toronto Photographer

By Brennan Suche  —  Toronto Wedding Photographer

The wedding day timeline is the most important planning document most couples don't give enough thought to. Not the seating chart. Not the florals. The timeline — because everything flows from it, and when it goes wrong, the photography suffers first.

After photographing weddings across Toronto and Ontario, I've seen timelines that gave the day room to breathe and timelines that were so compressed that nobody relaxed until after the ceremony. Here is how to build one that works.

Start With the Ceremony Time

Build your timeline backwards from the ceremony, and forwards from it. Your ceremony time is the fixed point around which everything else is arranged. Once you know when vows happen, you can work out getting ready start times, when portraits need to happen, and how much of the evening you have for reception coverage.

A common mistake is treating the ceremony as the starting point for planning and not accounting adequately for everything that has to happen before it. Getting ready — hair, makeup, getting dressed, family moments, first look if you're having one — takes significantly longer than most couples expect.

A Sample Toronto Wedding
Day Timeline

This is a framework for a 3:00 PM ceremony with a 5:00 PM reception. Adjust the anchor points for your ceremony time and travel between locations.

9:00 AM
Hair & Makeup BeginsFor a party of 4–6 people, plan 30–45 minutes per person. The person being photographed last goes last in the chair — not first. Confirm this with your hair and makeup team in advance.
11:30 AM
Photographer ArrivesI arrive while getting ready is still in progress — ideally with 60–90 minutes remaining. This gives time to document the details (dress, rings, florals), capture candid moments with your people, and photograph the final touches.
12:30 PM
Dressed & ReadyBuild in a buffer here. Getting dressed takes longer than expected, especially with a larger dress or complicated accessories. Being ready 30 minutes ahead of when you think you need to be is not a waste of time — it's the difference between a calm getting-ready exit and a rushed one.
1:00 PM
First Look (Optional)A private moment before the ceremony — just the two of you, with your photographer nearby. It dramatically reduces ceremony nerves, allows for relaxed couples portraits before the day gets busy, and frees up the cocktail hour for time with guests rather than portraits.
1:30 PM
Wedding Party PortraitsPlan 20–30 minutes for this. Keep the group manageable and give your photographer a list of the specific combinations you want — it makes the whole process faster for everyone.
2:15 PM
Travel to Ceremony VenueToronto traffic is real. Build in more buffer than you think you need, especially for Friday and Saturday afternoon travel between neighbourhoods. Parkside to Yorkville at 2 PM on a Saturday is not a 10-minute drive.
3:00 PM
CeremonyMost Toronto wedding ceremonies run 20–40 minutes. Religious ceremonies often run longer. Know your officiant's typical pace.
3:45 PM
Cocktail Hour BeginsYour guests go to cocktails. You and your partner have 20–30 minutes for couples portraits — ideally in soft afternoon light if your ceremony ends before 5 PM. This is often the most relaxed photography of the day.
4:30 PM
Family Formal PortraitsKeep your must-have list to 10–12 groupings maximum. Each takes 3–5 minutes. Assign someone — a coordinator, a family member — to gather people. Without a designated caller, this takes twice as long.
5:00 PM
Reception BeginsGrand entrance, first dance, toasts. The first hour of the reception tends to be the most photographically rich — energy is high and the room is still focused on you.
7:30 PM
Golden Hour PortraitsIf sunset is around 8–9 PM (spring/summer in Toronto), step out for 15–20 minutes for outdoor portraits in golden light. These are almost always among the favourite photos of the day. You won't miss much inside, and it's worth it.
10:00 PM
End of CoverageAn 8-hour package covering 11:30 AM to 7:30 PM captures the full arc of the day for most Toronto weddings. If you want late-night coverage, build that into your package from the start.

The Mistakes That
Derail Timelines

Underestimating Getting Ready

This is the most common timeline problem I see. Couples plan for hair and makeup to finish at noon and it finishes at 1:30 PM. Everything downstream compresses. The fix is simple: ask your hair and makeup team exactly how long they need, add 30 minutes, and build the rest of the timeline from there.

Not Accounting for Travel in Toronto

Google Maps gives you the optimistic estimate. Factor in parking, loading equipment, and the reality that your wedding party will not all be ready to leave at the same time. Budget 25–30% more travel time than the app suggests for any move between locations.

Cramming Too Many Portrait Groups

Family formals are important, but a list of 25 groupings will eat your cocktail hour and leave everyone — including you — exhausted. Curate it. The essential groupings are immediate family and a handful of combinations that genuinely matter to you. Everything else can often be captured candidly over the course of the evening.

Not Building in Buffer

A timeline with no slack is a timeline waiting to fail. Build 15–20 minutes of buffer into the getting-ready block, the portrait block, and the travel-to-ceremony block. If nothing goes wrong, you'll arrive relaxed. If something does, you'll have room to absorb it.

"The couples who enjoy their wedding day the most are almost always the ones with a well-built timeline and a planner or coordinator keeping it on track. Presence is only possible when you're not worried about being late."

Should You Have a First Look?

The first look — a private reveal before the ceremony — is one of the most meaningful decisions you'll make for your timeline and your photos. Arguments against it usually come down to tradition: wanting the aisle moment to be the first time you see each other. That's a valid reason to skip it.

But the practical case for a first look is strong. It allows for relaxed, unhurried couples portraits before the ceremony when you're fresh and not yet emotionally exhausted. It frees the cocktail hour. It gives you a private moment together before the day becomes public. And it consistently produces some of the most genuine photographs of the day.

There's no wrong answer. But if you're on the fence, I'd encourage you to try it.
