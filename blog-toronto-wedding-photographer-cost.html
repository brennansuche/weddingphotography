How Much Does a Wedding Photographer
Cost in Toronto?

By Brennan Suche  —  Toronto Wedding Photographer

Wedding photography pricing in Toronto is genuinely confusing. You can find photographers charging $1,500 and photographers charging $12,000, and without knowing what drives those numbers it's hard to know whether you're getting a deal or making a mistake.

This is an honest breakdown from someone who has been shooting weddings in Toronto for years. Not a sales pitch — just a clear look at what the market looks like, what drives the price, and how to decide what's right for you.

What Toronto Wedding Photographers
Charge in 2026

The honest market range for wedding photography in Toronto and the GTA runs roughly from $2,500 to $8,000+, with most experienced full-time photographers sitting between $3,500 and $6,000 for a full wedding day. Here's how that breaks down in practice:

Price Range What You're Typically Getting
Under $2,500 Newer photographers building their portfolio, part-time shooters, or photographers who haven't yet priced their work sustainably. You may get beautiful results — or you may not. The risk is higher.
$2,500 – $3,500 Early-career photographers with 1–3 years of experience and a growing body of work. Often talented. Less likely to have shot dozens of weddings across varied lighting conditions and timelines.
$3,500 – $5,500 The core of Toronto's professional market. Full-time photographers with established processes, consistent results, and the experience to handle unpredictable days. This is where most couples should be looking.
$5,500 – $8,000+ Photographers with significant editorial or editorial-adjacent work, strong reputations, and often a waitlist. The work is typically exceptional. Whether the premium is worth it depends entirely on how much photography matters to you.

What's Actually Included

Most Toronto wedding photographers price their services as packages, but the contents of those packages vary widely. When comparing quotes, these are the things that actually matter:

Hours of Coverage

Most full-day packages run 8–10 hours. This is usually enough to cover getting ready through the first dance or cake cutting. If you want end-of-night coverage — last dances, late-night snacks, the full arc of the evening — you'll either need a longer package or an overtime rate. Know what you need before you compare prices.

Edited Digital Gallery

The standard deliverable is a gallery of edited digital files. What varies is the number of images (300–800 is a normal range for a full day), the turnaround time (6–14 weeks is typical), and whether the files are delivered at full resolution or web-optimized. Always clarify resolution and download rights — you want to be able to print without restrictions.

Second Photographer

A second shooter adds meaningful coverage, particularly during getting-ready moments when the couple is in separate locations, and during ceremonies where one photographer can cover the aisle while the other covers the altar. Some packages include a second shooter; others charge $400–$800 to add one. Worth the money for most weddings over 80 guests.

Engagement Session

Many photographers offer an engagement session as part of their wedding package or as an add-on. Beyond the obvious benefit of portrait photos, an engagement session is valuable because it gives you time together before the wedding day — when the stakes are lower — to get comfortable in front of the camera and build trust with your photographer.

Albums and Prints

Physical albums are typically priced separately, ranging from $800 to $2,500+ for a high-quality heirloom album. If you care about a physical product, factor this into your overall photography budget rather than treating it as an optional extra.

What Makes Some Photographers
More Expensive

Beyond experience, a few specific things tend to push prices up for Toronto wedding photographers.

Demand is the biggest one. Photographers who are consistently booked 18–24 months out can — and should — charge more than photographers with open weekends. If someone you love is already fully booked, that's often a signal, not a reason to look elsewhere.

Specialization matters too. Photographers who have built a strong body of work in a specific style — documentary, editorial, film — tend to charge a premium because they've done the work to become genuinely good at something specific. Generalists are often cheaper; specialists are often worth more.

Finally, the hidden costs of quality add up. Full-frame professional camera bodies, backup equipment, professional editing software, insurance, and ongoing education are real expenses that professional photographers factor into their pricing. Photographers charging substantially less than the market often haven't accounted for these costs yet — which raises questions about their long-term sustainability as a business.

How to Think About
Your Budget

The standard advice is to allocate 10–15% of your total wedding budget to photography. That's a reasonable starting point, but it misses something important: photographs are the only part of your wedding day that lasts. The flowers are gone. The cake is gone. The dress is in a box. The photographs are what you'll actually look at for the rest of your life.

"I've never met a couple who said they spent too much on photography. I've met plenty who wish they'd spent more — or who cut their budget in an area that genuinely mattered to them."

The right budget is the one that gets you a photographer whose work genuinely moves you, whose style matches how you want your day to feel, and who you actually want to spend one of the most intense days of your life alongside. Start there, and work backward to the number.
