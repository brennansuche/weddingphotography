Wedding Photo Posing Tips
For Couples Who Hate Posing

By Brennan Suche  —  Toronto Wedding Photographer

Most people who say they're not photogenic are actually just people who've never had someone help them feel comfortable in front of a camera. The photographs that look effortless — the ones where the couple looks completely like themselves — aren't usually the result of perfect posing. They're the result of the right conditions: good light, a little direction, and enough trust to stop thinking about being photographed.

These are the things I tell every couple before we start. Not a list of poses to memorize, but a set of ideas that tend to make the whole experience better.

"There's no such thing as a bad subject — only subjects who haven't been given the right direction yet. My job is to put you somewhere with good light and give you enough to work with so you can stop thinking about the camera."

Before We Even
Start Shooting

1. Do an engagement session

Tip 01

The single most effective thing you can do to improve your wedding day portraits is spend an hour with your photographer before the wedding. An engagement session isn't just about engagement photos — it's about learning how your photographer works, figuring out what directions feel natural to you, and getting comfortable enough in front of a lens that you stop noticing it's there.

Couples who skip the engagement session and meet their photographer for the first time on the wedding morning are starting from zero on the most important day. Couples who've already done a session together arrive at portraits already warmed up, already trusting, already knowing what to expect. The difference in the photographs is visible every time.

2. Look at your photographer's full galleries, not just the highlights

Tip 02

Before you book anyone, ask to see complete galleries from recent weddings — not just a curated portfolio. You want to know that you'll look like yourself in the photographs, not just that the photographer can produce beautiful images of photogenic people in ideal conditions. Full galleries show you how a photographer handles the real shape of a day: mixed light, nervous moments, the in-between seconds. That's what matters.

During Portraits

3. Move toward each other, not at the camera

Tip 03

The stiffest portraits almost always happen when both people are facing the camera directly and thinking about how they look. The most natural portraits almost always happen when the couple is paying attention to each other instead. When I ask you to walk, lean in, whisper something, or just look at each other — I'm not being vague. I'm redirecting your attention away from the lens and toward the person next to you. Let that happen. The camera will take care of itself.

4. What to do with your hands

Tip 04

"What do I do with my hands?" is the question I hear most often. The answer is almost always: hold something, or hold each other. Idle hands read as stiff in photographs because they are stiff — hands with nothing to do default to tension. The fixes are simple:

  • Hold hands, loosely — not a death grip, just contact
  • One person puts their hand on the other's chest, waist, or the back of their neck
  • Walk together with arms around each other
  • One person holds the other's face during a close moment
  • If all else fails, put your hands in your pockets — it relaxes the shoulders and looks intentional

The goal is not a specific hand configuration. The goal is to give your hands something to do so you stop thinking about them.

5. Lean in — further than feels natural

Tip 05

Almost every couple, when asked to get close together, stops about 10% short of where they should be. What feels like close contact in person often reads as a polite gap in a photograph. When I ask you to lean in or get closer, trust that I can see what the frame looks like — go a little further than feels instinctive. The photographs where couples look genuinely connected are almost always the ones where they're actually touching.

6. Slow down

Tip 06

When I ask you to walk toward me, or turn toward each other, or move through a space — do it at about half the speed that feels normal. Fast movement blurs. It also reads as nervous. Slow movement gives me time to find the frame, and it tends to look deliberate and graceful rather than hurried. If you feel like you're moving in slow motion, you're probably moving at exactly the right pace.

7. Let your eyes close or look away

Tip 07

Staring directly into a camera lens for a sustained period is uncomfortable for most people, and photographs taken during that sustained stare often look uncomfortable. Looking away — at each other, at the middle distance, at something in the environment — takes the pressure off and often produces more interesting photographs than direct eye contact does. Some of the best portraits happen with eyes closed: during a forehead touch, a quiet moment, a held breath before something begins.

8. Laugh at something real

Tip 08

Forced smiles are one of the easiest things to spot in a photograph. Genuine ones are one of the easiest things to capture, but only if there's actually something to smile about. If something genuinely strikes you as funny during portraits — something I say, something that goes slightly wrong, something your partner does — let yourself laugh. Don't suppress it to get back to the "right" expression. The laughing photograph is almost always the one you'll come back to.

On the Wedding Day

9. Build a timeline with breathing room

Tip 09

Rushed portraits are almost always inferior portraits — not because of technique, but because couples who are watching the clock can't fully relax. The best portraits happen when neither of you is thinking about where you need to be in 10 minutes. If you want photographs that feel unhurried, build unhurried time into your wedding day timeline. Twenty to thirty minutes for couples portraits after the ceremony, plus a golden hour break during the reception if possible, is usually enough.

10. Trust your photographer

Tip 10

This sounds simple but it matters more than any specific tip. If you've chosen a photographer whose work resonates with you and spent time together before the wedding, the hardest part is already done. On the day itself, when we're doing portraits, let yourself follow direction without second-guessing how you look. Your photographer is looking at the frame. You don't need to. The couples who look best in their photographs are almost always the ones who stopped worrying and started being present with the person they were marrying.

A Note on "Not Being Photogenic"

I've heard this from almost every couple I've worked with, at least once. And I genuinely believe it's almost never true. What people mean when they say they're not photogenic is usually one of a few things: they've been photographed badly, they've been photographed in conditions that didn't suit them, or they've never had someone take the time to make them comfortable before pointing a camera at them.

Good photographs of people aren't primarily the result of how those people look. They're the result of how those people feel — and that's something you can actually do something about. That's what the engagement session is for. That's what the preparation is for. That's what showing up with some trust and some patience is for.

If you want to talk about your wedding day or ask about how I work with couples who've never done this before, reach out. That conversation is always easy.
